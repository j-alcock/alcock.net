Famous Alcocks

The name is not common, but it keeps excellent company. A selection of Alcocks who left their mark.

The headliner

  • Sir John Alcock1892–1919 · aviator

    On 14–15 June 1919, Captain John Alcock and navigator Arthur Whitten Brown flew a modified Vickers Vimy bomber from St. John's, Newfoundland, to a bog near Clifden, Ireland — the first nonstop transatlantic flight, roughly 1,900 miles in about sixteen hours, through fog and sleet in an open cockpit. Both men were knighted by King George V. Alcock died just six months later in a flying accident in France, aged 27. “Alcock and Brown” remains one of the most famous pairings in aviation history — eight years before Charles Lindbergh's solo crossing.

    Source: Wikipedia

Church & state

  • John Alcockc. 1430–1500 · bishop & founder

    Bishop of Rochester, Worcester, and finally Ely; twice Lord Chancellor of England. In 1496 he founded Jesus College, Cambridge, whose arms still bear the cocks' heads of his punning rebus. A noted builder and educator of his age.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Sir Rutherford Alcock1809–1897 · diplomat

    Surgeon turned diplomat who became the first British minister resident in Japan (1859) as the country opened to the West. He wrote The Capital of the Tycoon, championed Japanese art in Europe, and in 1860 made the first recorded ascent of Mount Fuji by a non-Japanese person.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Reg Alcock1948–2011 · politician

    Canadian Member of Parliament and President of the Treasury Board, known for championing modernization of government services.

    Source: Wikipedia

Sport

  • Charles W. “C.W.” Alcock1842–1907 · sportsman & administrator

    Secretary of the Football Association who in 1871 proposed a knockout competition open to all clubs: the FA Cup, the oldest football competition in the world. He captained the Wanderers to victory in its first final (1872), helped arrange the first international football match (England v Scotland, 1872), and as secretary of Surrey County Cricket Club staged the first Test cricket match played in England (The Oval, 1880). Arguably no one did more to invent modern organized sport.

    Source: Wikipedia

Science & scholarship

  • George Alcock1912–2000 · amateur astronomer

    A schoolteacher from Peterborough who became one of the most successful visual discoverers in astronomy, finding five comets and five novae — most from his back garden or, in one famous case, spotting comet IRAS–Araki–Alcock (1983) through a closed window. He memorized tens of thousands of star positions to do it.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Charles R. Alcockb. 1951 · astrophysicist

    Led the MACHO Project searching for dark matter via gravitational microlensing, and served as director of the Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Alfred William Alcock1859–1933 · naturalist

    Physician-naturalist of British India, superintendent of the Indian Museum in Calcutta, and prolific describer of deep-sea fauna from the pioneering surveys of RIMS Investigator.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Leslie Alcock1925–2006 · archaeologist

    One of Britain's leading early-medieval archaeologists. His excavations at Cadbury Castle — a candidate for Camelot — and his book Arthur's Britain shaped how a generation thought about post-Roman Britain.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Benjamin Alcock1801–1865 · anatomist

    Irish anatomist, first professor of anatomy at Queen's College Cork, where he lost his chair for refusing to procure famine victims' corpses for dissection in defiance of the Anatomy Act — a principled stand that ended his career and sent him to America. Medical students everywhere still learn the pudendal canal by its other name: Alcock's canal.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Nathaniel Graham Alcock1881–1953 · urologist

    American surgeon — “the Chief” — who founded the Department of Urology at the University of Iowa in 1923 and built it into one of the most respected programs in the country, pioneering transurethral prostate surgery in the 1930s.

    Source: University of Iowa Archives · PubMed

  • James Alcockb. 1942 · psychologist

    Canadian psychologist and a leading scientific skeptic, known for his work on the psychology of belief and anomalous experience.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Rachel Alcock1862–1939 · physiologist

    English physiologist at a time when very few women worked in the field, publishing research from the Cambridge physiological laboratory.

    Source: Wikipedia

Music, letters & screen

  • John Alcock1715–1806 · organist & composer

    Organist of Lichfield Cathedral, doctor of music at Oxford, and composer of church music and glees still sung today. (Yes — a second famous John Alcock. There are several. It's a strong name.)

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Sir Walter Galpin Alcock1861–1947 · organist

    Organist of Salisbury Cathedral who played at three coronations — Edward VII (1902), George V (1911), and George VI (1937) — a distinction unlikely ever to be repeated.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Mary Alcockc. 1742–1798 · poet

    English poet and essayist whose satirical verse skewered the fashions and follies of the late eighteenth century.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Deborah Alcock1835–1913 · novelist

    Irish-born author of widely-read historical fiction, including The Spanish Brothers.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Vivien Alcock1924–2003 · children's author

    British writer of more than twenty novels for young readers, beginning with The Haunting of Cassie Palmer.

    Source: Wikipedia

  • Milly Alcockb. 2000 · actress

    Australian actress who broke out worldwide as the young Princess Rhaenyra Targaryen in House of the Dragon, and carries the name into its eighth century of public life as DC's Supergirl.

    Source: Wikipedia

The name in unexpected places

Beyond the people: Alcock Island off the Antarctic Peninsula, Alcock's canal in every anatomy textbook, the Alcock and Brown memorials at Heathrow and Clifden, and Alcock v Chief Constable of South Yorkshire Police, a case every English law student reads. The name gets around.
