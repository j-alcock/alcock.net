Welcome, Alcocks & friends

This site is devoted to the Alcock surname: where it came from, the remarkable people who have carried it, and the Alcocks carrying it today.

The name has been on the record since at least 1275, when one Alexander Alecoc appeared in the Subsidy Rolls of Worcestershire. Since then, Alcocks have founded a Cambridge college, invented the FA Cup, opened diplomatic relations with Japan, discovered comets from a back garden, and — most famously — flown an open-cockpit biplane nonstop across the Atlantic Ocean, eight years before Lindbergh.

Not bad for a name that started out meaning “little Al.”

Why the rooster?

The bird is the name's oldest badge. John Alcock, the 15th-century Bishop of Ely, signed his work with a rebus — a visual pun — of a cock standing on a globe, and the arms of Jesus College, Cambridge, which he founded in 1496, carry cocks' heads to this day. Many Alcock coats of arms are similarly “canting”: they picture the name. We keep the tradition going.
