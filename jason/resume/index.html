The journey

My dad taught me to solder at 6, I taught myself to code at 8, and I built my first 300 baud modem in 1980. I was dialing up the world before most people had ever used a computer, and I'd already broken and fixed mine countless times.

My mother said I started breaking things when my hands started working. It's (usually) curiosity rather than maliciousness. I'm not the kind of tester for whom things just start misbehaving when they enter the room; I'm the other kind. I can look at something, in the world or in code, and see where it's weakest, where it will break first, and the fastest way to make that happen. Then I take glee in watching it happen. That's the dopamine reward cycle in the mind of a tester.

My career started in the Bay Area just before the dot-com years, honed by a CS education focused on systems (LISP) and Verification and Validation. Two years managing an Apple Value Added Reseller storefront taught me what I wanted: to make computers and the Internet easier to use for as many people as possible. I know, an idealist. Still am.

I got lucky on my early bosses and carry their lessons to this day. “Don't be wrong about being right.” “People, Process, Technology, Money. Preferably in that order.” And from Gary, who had managed line repair crews for Pacific Gas and Electric for 40 years: “When you become a manager, you will make more and worse mistakes than you ever thought possible.” He promoted me soon after that pearl, and I've passed it on to everyone I've trained into leadership.

People I met at a Net Night, playing Bungie's Marathon (the first one), told me about an open role at Apple. I gave notice to Gary when I asked for the day off for the interview. I started as a contract tester and ended up a Testing Supervisor and MacOS Server Quality DRI with a team of 43, with Developers and Testers working side-by-side along the way. I still laugh at that very personal model being called eXtreme Programming.

When the 11th layoff at Apple finally caught me, the startup world called. Organic Online, selling Starbucks's first pound of coffee and HomeDepot's first bucket. Communities.com, working with Doug Crockford on the first JavaScript server, hosting a Britney Spears chat for hundreds of 2D avatars. Bigstep.com, small business ecommerce before Amazon allowed third-party sellers. When the bubble deflated I went north to Seattle and kept doing what I do: go where the product needs someone who cares whether it works, whether that's the AT&T Wireless billing system for the iPhone launch or a six-thousand-line sales tool at a five-person startup.

At GameHouse I tested dozens of games on hundreds of mobile handsets: play and work at the same time, and I never really gave it back. Wetpaint came through a friend (who would eventually officiate my wedding), rebuilding QA across 27 release cycles of a deceptively complex wiki platform. At Appature I ran quality, production operations, and customer success simultaneously, years a colleague summed up as being “our customers' best employee that they don't know they have.”

AudienceScience brought my toughest quality challenges: advertising for the world's largest advertiser at up to 140 billion transactions a day with a 3ms, 5 9s SLA. We took the release process from an overnight behemoth for a dozen people to something one person ran in the middle of the day, and built the CI/CD pipeline to match. Oh, and we kept that SLA the entire time.

Something else I started at an early age was playing D&D, so I cried when WotC called. Four years leading global SDET teams behind the digital tools of Magic: The Gathering and Dungeons & Dragons. Test coverage quadrupled, escaped defects fell 90%, and I was rated highest among engineering managers by employees two years running. That is the number I'm proudest of, because my success is predicated on my team's. After Wizards, DexCare put that discipline to work where the stakes are appointments with doctors, not dice.

I still build as much as I test: reinforcement-learning trading systems, agent-operated business infrastructure, published World of Warcraft addons, and a quality consultancy, OurQA, for teams that want their testing practice looked at hard. I'm a tester at heart, as well as a gamer. Very few days go by without me doing both.

Quality has changed dramatically in the last year. We can write, test, and ship more code at higher quality than ever before. Yes, testing can improve with agentic tools. It has to be intentional and it has to be done with knowledge. I'm having a great time playing with it.
